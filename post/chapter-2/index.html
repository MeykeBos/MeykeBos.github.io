Customer Sentiment Analysis

April 25, 2021

As our final assignment for the Techionista Academy, we had to create a Power BI report to analyse the customer sentiment for a Dutch company. Unfortunately, we have been asked to be careful with the data we share since it contains privacy sensitive information. Therefore, I won’t be able to share my code or the Power BI report itself. Instead, I will share screenshots of the result.

The data that was provided consisted of the following columns:

  • Answer date
  • Comment
  • Gender
  • Age group
  • Language
  • City
  • Customer concept

In order to extract a sentiment score out of the comments column, I used Microsoft Cognitive Services. It returns a score between 0 and 1. One being completely happy with the service and zero being completely unhappy with the service. Just for the fun of it, I also created a small sentiment classification model on top of SpaCy that classified all comments into one of the following groups: unhappy, neutral, happy. I might write another post about my own findings in comparison to the Microsoft Cognitive Services in the future.

I focused the report on two main topics: analysing the average sentiment score for the various customer segments on the one hand, and on the other hand the topics that were addressed most often in the comments section, and how these topics influence the sentiment score.

But before going into these two topics, lets first look at the homepage of the dashboard. On the homepage, I show some figures that give an overview of the customers that are included within the report. As you can see, the report is based on a 1000 reviews. The average sentiment score of those 1000 people is 0.40. In the three bar charts you can see the distribution of the observations by Age, City Size, and Gender. With the two buttons, you can navigate either to look at the customer sentiment analysis, or the sentiment influencers.

The analysis part consists of three pages: sentiment over time, sentiment by city, and sentiment by age group. I choose to keep all three tabs similar with regards to looks and filtering options. On the left hand side you see four cards. The top most card shows the average sentiment of the selection that you make with the filtering options on the right side of the page. The other three cards show the percentage of the selection that gave either a positive, a negative or a neutral review.

SENTIMENT OVER TIME

The graph in the middle shows the amount of reviews by day (blue barchart), the average sentiment score per day (dark blue graph) and the moving average (orange graph) that is now set to the moving average of 7 days. You can change the amount of days for the moving average in the right bottom corner (0-31 days).

SENTIMENT BY CITY

This time, the middle of the page shows a map of all cities of the reviews. The size of the bubble represents the amount of reviews for that particular city. The color of the bubble represents the whether the average sentiment score for the city is positive (= green), neutral (= blue), or negative (= red). Again you can change the selection of reviews you want to focus on with the filters on the right hand side.

SENTIMENT BY AGE

Lastly, this analysis page shows the average sentiment score per age group. As you can see the people between 51 and 64 years old are in general most satisfied.

INFLUENCERS

The last page of the dashboard shows the topics that were mentioned most often in the comments section. The graph shows what happends to the average sentiment score when one of these topics is mentioned (in comparison to not mentioned). For example, if people mention ‘lessen’, they on average have a slightly higher sentiment score then people that do not mention ‘lessen’. Since you can filter on a specific city, managers of a certain city can see right away what they can focus on to improve their average sentiment score.
